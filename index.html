The Concept

Series Overview

In an exclusive, tight-knit community of affluent, culturally diverse friends, a gay married couple's decision to have another child through surrogacy — with one of their closest friends — sets off a chain of emotional, cultural, and relational reckonings that will redefine what family actually means.

Something Like Family is a premium, emotionally driven reality series that lives at the intersection of modern love, chosen family, and the real complexity of building a life on your own terms.

At its center: Christian & Curtis — a gay married couple whose opposing personalities create the show's fundamental tension. Christian leads with his heart. Curtis leads with his ledger. Together, they're deciding whether to have another child — and whether their closest friend, Moloko, is the person to carry it.

Running through every frame is Haile — perceptive, strategic, and grounded — who becomes the quiet force the group keeps returning to when decisions get too real.

This is not a show about drama. It's a show about decisions. And the relationships that survive them.

01
Who He Is
Christian is the person who makes the group feel like a group. He texts first. He plans the dinners. He remembers everyone's birthdays. His warmth is not performance — it's constitution. In his family, love was loud, food was always on the table, and you showed up for people. He has built his adult life on the same principle.
What Drives Him
Christian's desire to have another child is not frivolous. It comes from a specific understanding of what family gave him — and what he wants to give forward. He grew up with a big, loud, present family, and the absence of that same scale in his own household is a quiet ache he doesn't always name. Surrogacy, to Christian, is not a workaround — it's an act of love.
His Tension
"His emotional leadership style sometimes outpaces his partner's need for process — and the gap between them becomes visible under pressure."
Relationships Within the Group
With Curtis: He knows Curtis loves him. He also knows that Curtis processes everything — including love — through logic and structure. Christian has learned to interpret Curtis's caution as care. Most of the time.

With Moloko: The kind of friendship built on direct honesty. She'll tell him when he's wrong. He'll tell her when she's being stubborn. The surrogacy conversation is the most significant test of that honesty.

With Haile: Christian trusts Haile's read on situations. He brings her things he doesn't bring to Curtis — not because he's hiding them, but because sometimes you need a different kind of perspective.
Season 1 Arc
Episode 1: Leading with desireEpisode 8: Leading with understanding
Christian starts the season leading with desire and ends it with understanding. He doesn't get everything he wants on the timeline he wanted it — but he gets something more valuable: clarity about who his partner is and what their friendship infrastructure can actually hold.

Who He Is
Curtis is not cold — he's precise. There is a difference, and the show's job is to reveal that difference. Curtis has built their life through intentionality. Every asset, every relationship, every commitment was stress-tested before it was made. This isn't fear — it's a philosophy. He's watched people he loves make decisions from emotion and pay for it for years.
What Drives Him
Curtis's love for Christian is complete and consistent. But he expresses it through action, not declaration. He protects Christian from the things Christian doesn't see coming. He builds the structures that let Christian be free. The tension between Christian's emotional expressiveness and Curtis's measured pragmatism is not a conflict — it's a marriage. It just looks different under pressure.
His Tension
"His pragmatism can read as coldness to people who don't know him well — including, sometimes, the cameras. The show's job is to reveal what's underneath."
The Surrogacy Question — From Curtis's Perspective
For Curtis, the surrogacy conversation isn't about whether they want another child. It's about whether they can sustain what they already have while adding something this large. His hesitation is not a rejection — it's a request for a plan. The cameras will catch him trying to find one.
Season 1 Arc
Episode 1: The obstacleEpisode 8: The foundation
Curtis begins the season as the obstacle and ends it as the foundation. The audience's understanding of him shifts as the cameras catch him in unguarded moments — where his love for his husband and his terror of losing what they've built are equally visible and equally real.

03
Who She Is
Moloko doesn't perform for anyone. She's already done the hardest things in life — and she has the receipts. Her South African identity is inseparable from her sense of self. Ubuntu — the Southern African philosophy that translates as "I am because we are" — is not a concept she recites. It's the operating system she runs on.
The South African Cultural Lens
In many South African cultural traditions, the act of bearing a child carries profound spiritual and familial weight — it is not simply a biological transaction. There are questions, unspoken but present, about what this means for her lineage, her body, her standing in her own family's eyes. The show doesn't lecture about this. It watches her live it.
Her Tension
"How do you hold your own family's needs, your cultural identity, and your loyalty to a friendship — all at the same time?"
Her Children — The First Consideration
Moloko's decision about the surrogacy isn't just about Christian and Curtis. It's about what her children would understand about what their mother did — and why. That conversation, on camera, is unlike anything unscripted television has produced.
Season 1 Arc
Episode 1: Supporting presenceEpisode 8: The show's moral center
Moloko's decision — whatever it is — is the most significant act of the season. Her "yes" or "no" is the central dramatic question. The audience will have earned the right to understand it.

Who She Is
Haile is not the loudest person in the room — but she's the one everyone looks at when the conversation gets complicated. She sees what's coming before it arrives. She understands group dynamics the way a chess player understands the board. Her perceptiveness is a gift that sometimes feels like a burden.

Raised between Los Angeles and Michigan, Haile carries both coasts in her. LA gave her cultural fluency, access, and the ability to read a room full of different kinds of people instantly. Michigan gave her groundedness, directness, and the ability to cut through performance to find the truth. She moves between the two with ease — which is, in retrospect, exactly what this friend group needs.
Her Role
"She can hold space for Moloko's South African perspective, meet Christian in his emotional language, and translate it all back to Curtis without anyone feeling talked past."
The Household Experiment
Haile's brief foray into a more formalized support role in Christian and Curtis's household isn't a failure — it's a lesson. She learns where her limits are. She learns what friendship can hold and what it can't. She comes out of it with more clarity, not less.

Season 1 · 8 Episodes

The Arc

Each episode is structured around a decision, a revelation, or a reckoning. The surrogacy question is the spine — but every episode deepens the friendship architecture beneath it.

Episode 01
"The Circle"
Establish the world. Establish the stakes.
We enter through a group gathering — the kind of effortless social ritual that masks how precisely curated it is. By the end, Christian makes a comment, half-joke, half-genuine, about wanting another child. The table reacts. The season begins.
Tone: Warm · A little too perfect
Episode 02
"The Idea"
From casual comment to real conversation.
Christian brings surrogacy into an intentional conversation with Curtis. What was a throwaway comment is now on the table — and Curtis's reaction reveals everything. Meanwhile, Moloko hears about it. Her response is not what anyone expected.
Tone: First real tension enters
Episode 03
"What We Carry"
Cultural weight surfaces.
As the surrogacy conversation deepens, the cultural dimensions become impossible to ignore. Moloko's South African framework — what it means to carry a child — shapes every instinct she has. Christian's vision of family and Curtis's vision of legacy collide for the first time.
Tone: Deeper · More personal
Episode 04
"Structured Life"
What it takes to hold all of this together.
The reality of the household comes into focus — what it costs to maintain the lifestyle, the structure, the schedule. Haile begins informally helping. Curtis's need for control becomes clearer. The question is: can their systems handle more?
Tone: Grounded · Slightly comedic

The Reckoning

The back half of the season is where the question becomes unavoidable — and every character has to decide what they're willing to risk for what they love.

What the Show Is Really About

Core Themes

01
Modern Family Building
Surrogacy as a real, complex, emotionally loaded decision — not a storyline device. What does it mean to ask a friend to carry your child? What does it mean to say yes? What does it mean to say no, and still love someone after?
02
The Architecture of Friendship
Close friendship, at its highest level, requires negotiation. Roles, limits, availability, expectation — friendships are their own kind of contract, and this show explores what happens when those contracts are tested by something irreversible.
03
Culture as Character
South African ubuntu and Black American pragmatism in the same room — shaping how characters understand family, obligation, tradition, and identity. Not explained. Witnessed. This is multicultural not as a casting note, but as a lived reality.
04
Emotional Intelligence as Power
In a media landscape full of reactive personalities, these characters are highly emotionally intelligent — and that becomes its own form of drama. Watching people who are genuinely trying to get it right is more compelling than watching people who aren't trying at all.
05
The Gap Between the Life You Show and the Life You Live
Every character has built something beautiful. The tension comes from maintaining it — and deciding whether to risk it for something even bigger. The aspirational exterior is real. So is what it costs.

Expansion

Beyond Season 1

Season 1 ends with a decision made and a friendship recalibrated. The format is built to grow — in depth, in cast, and in geography.

Season 2 — Path A
If Moloko proceeds: The pregnancy. The cultural rituals. The couple's preparation. The friendship during the most intimate experience of all their lives. The show becomes something that has never existed on American television.
What it looks like to choose each other even when the stakes are biological. A season about love expressed through sacrifice — and whether that kind of love changes everything.
Season 2 — Path B
If she doesn't: The group navigates what that decision meant. What the couple does next. How a friendship holds — or bends — after saying no to something that mattered that much.
Not every no is a betrayal. This path explores how relationships absorb disappointment — and what it means to stay when you could leave.
Franchise Potential
The community format is replicable. Atlanta. Houston. Miami. Different cities, same cultural intelligence, new casts — built on the same emotional framework. Something Like Family is not a one-city show. It's a model for how premium unscripted can travel. The IP is the format, not the cast. The cast is the proof of concept.
